# Mathematical Theory of the Triple Dragon Fractal

This repository implements the high-performance visualization of the Triple Dragon Fractal, a beautiful chaotic structure arising from the iteration of a perturbed rational function on the complex plane $\mathbb{C}$.

The project contains a high-performance multi-threaded Rust renderer (triple_dragon.rs) that exports pixel-perfect PNG images natively.

## 1. The Core Formula

The Triple Dragon is defined by the discrete dynamical system generated by iterating a perturbed rational map. As shown in the reference file equation.png, the recurrence relation of state is:

$$z_{n+1} = f(z_n) = \frac{z_n^3}{z_n^3 + 1} + c$$

Where:

$z_n \in \mathbb{C}$ is the complex coordinate state at step $n$.

$c \in \mathbb{C}$ is a constant complex parameter that acts as a perturbation seed.

$z_0$ represents the initial position of a given pixel mapped onto the complex coordinate plane.

## 2. Fixed Points, Attractors, and Poles

To understand why the Triple Dragon forms three main symmetrical bodies, we analyze the unperturbed map ($c = 0$):

$$g(z) = \frac{z^3}{z^3 + 1}$$

Singularities (Poles)

The map possesses poles where the denominator vanishes:

$$z^3 + 1 = 0 \implies z = e^{i\pi(2k+1)/3} \quad \text{for } k \in \{0, 1, 2\}$$

These three poles lie on the unit circle at:

$p_0 = \frac{1}{2} + i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

$p_1 = -1$

$p_2 = \frac{1}{2} - i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$

When an iteration orbit lands near these points, the map divides by zero, sending the trajectory to infinity ($\infty$).

Attractors (Fixed Points)

The stable fixed points of the unperturbed system satisfy $g(z) = z$:

$$\frac{z^3}{z^3 + 1} = z \implies z^3 = z(z^3 + 1) \implies z^4 - z^3 + z = 0$$

Factoring this polynomial yields:

$$z(z^3 - z^2 + 1) = 0$$

This gives four fixed points:

$z = 0$: Since the derivative $g'(z) = \frac{3z^2}{(z^3+1)^2}$ is exactly zero at $z=0$, the origin is a superattracting fixed point.

Three roots of the cubic $z^3 - z^2 + 1 = 0$: One real root at $z \approx -0.755$ and two complex conjugate roots at $z \approx 0.877 \pm 0.745i$.

Due to the $z^3$ term, the map exhibits a three-fold rotational symmetry ($120^\circ$ rotation). There are three primary finite basins of attraction corresponding to the attracting fixed points, plus a fourth basin of attraction at infinity ($\infty$).

## 3. The Wada Basin Property

One of the most remarkable topological features of the Triple Dragon fractal is the Wada Property.

In classical complex dynamics (like the Mandelbrot or standard Julia sets), boundaries usually separate exactly two basins of attraction. However, for certain rational maps of degree $\geq 3$ (including the Triple Dragon):

$$\partial B_1 = \partial B_2 = \partial B_3 = J$$

Where $B_1, B_2, B_3$ are the basins of attraction for the three distinct finite fixed points, and $J$ is the chaotic Julia set boundary.

This means that every single point on the boundary of one color basin is also on the boundary of the other two color basins. If you stand on the edge of the blue basin, you are simultaneously touching the red and green basins. This boundary is infinitely folded, producing the intricate, self-similar "dragon limbs" visible in the plots.

## 4. Framing, Zoom, and the Complex Plane Viewport

In initial test renders, choosing an oversized coordinate window (such as a zoom factor of $3.0$) resulted in a tiny fractal surrounded by massive margins of empty space, as shown in triple_dragon_pastel.jpg.

To resolve this and achieve the perfect framing of the original masterpieces, the viewport window must be narrowed down.

By setting the coordinate boundary to $[-1.25, 1.25]$ on both axes (a zoom factor of exactly $1.25$), the viewport tightly crops the active boundary limits.

This allows the delicate outer spiral tips of the limbs to extend cleanly to the edges of the canvas, matching the reference images dragon8_s_2.png and dragon1_s.jpg precisely.

## 5. Color Mapping Mechanics

The rendering engine converts convergence speeds into beautiful gradients using a convergence test:

$$\Delta z = \vert{}z_{n+1} - z_n\vert{} < \epsilon$$

If $\Delta z$ drops below a tiny threshold $\epsilon$ (typically $10^{-5}$), the orbit is considered to have settled into one of its attractors. The iteration step $i$ at which this occurs is normalized:

$$t_{\text{linear}} = \frac{i}{i_{\text{max}}}$$

### A. Classic Monochrome

To obtain the crisp, high-contrast off-white background from dragon8_s_2.png, the normalized iteration is processed through a non-linear power curve:

$$\text{Gray} = 0.96 - 0.96 \cdot (t_{\text{linear}})^{\gamma}$$

Where the contrast scale $\gamma \approx 3.2$.

Rapid convergence (e.g., $i \approx 0$ in the flat background regions) yields $t_{\text{linear}} \approx 0$, mapping to a bright, clean off-white value of $0.96$.

Slow convergence near the chaotic boundary yields $t_{\text{linear}} \to 1$, dropping the gray value down to deep charcoal or pitch black ($0.0$).

### B. Pastel Coral Sky

For the multi-colored pastel theme, the normalized value $t_{\text{linear}}$ is mapped across a segmented linear interpolation (LERP) ramp:

Backdrop Sky Blue: $t_{\text{linear}} \in [0.0, 0.22]$ interpolates from light sky-blue RGB(212, 240, 253) to pale peach RGB(253, 232, 232).

Basin Peach: $t_{\text{linear}} \in [0.22, 0.70]$ interpolates from pale peach to a warm coral salmon RGB(250, 143, 128).

Boundary Transition: $t_{\text{linear}} \in [0.70, 1.00]$ interpolates from coral salmon into a deep, mysterious indigo-violet boundary shade RGB(71, 69, 130).

This creates a high-fidelity visual translation of the underlying chaotic math.
